### **Section 1: Foundational Concepts (Introduction, Classification, and Nomenclature)**

This section establishes the basic definitions and structural types covered in the unit:

1. **Introduction and Scope:** Defining **Haloalkanes** (alkyl halides) where the halogen is attached to an $sp^3$ hybridised carbon atom of an alkyl group, and **Haloarenes** (aryl halides) where the halogen is attached to an $sp^2$ hybridised carbon atom of an aryl group. The chapter also covers the required portion of **Optical Isomerism**.
2. **Classification:**
    - **Based on the Number of Halogen Atoms:** Compounds are classified as **mono**, **di**, or **polyhalogen** (tri-, tetra-, etc.) derivatives.
    - **Compounds Containing $\mathbf{sp^3}$ C—X Bond:** Includes **Alkyl halides** (1°, 2°, 3°), **Allylic halides** (halogen bonded to an $sp^3$-hybridized carbon adjacent to a C=C double bond), and **Benzylic halides** (halogen bonded to an $sp^3$-hybridized carbon attached to an aromatic ring).
    - **Compounds Containing $\mathbf{sp^2}$ C—X Bond:** Includes **Vinylic halides** (halogen bonded directly to an $sp^2$-hybridized carbon of a C=C double bond), and **Aryl halides** (halogen directly bonded to an $sp^2$-hybridized carbon of an aromatic ring).
    - **Special Classification of Dihalides:** Distinguished as **geminal** (both halogens on the same carbon) or **vicinal** (halogens on adjacent carbons).
3. **Nomenclature:** Discussing both common names and **IUPAC naming conventions** for alkyl and aryl halides, noting that IUPAC is the primary focus for questions.

### **Section 2: Physical Properties and Stereochemical Aspects**

This section deals with the physical characteristics of the compounds and introduces the stereochemistry required for understanding reaction mechanisms:

1. **Nature of the C–X Bond:** Analyzing the polarity of the C–X bond due to the greater electronegativity of the halogen atom.
2. **Physical Properties:**
    - **Melting and Boiling Points:** They are generally higher than comparable hydrocarbons due to stronger **van der Waals forces** and dipole-dipole interactions. Boiling point increases with **molecular mass** (RI > RBr > RCl > RF) and decreases with **increased branching**. The **para-isomers** of dihalobenzenes have higher melting points due to greater **symmetry** and better fitting in the crystal lattice.
    - **Density:** Bromo, iodo, and polychloro derivatives are heavier than water, and density increases with **increasing mass of the halogen atom**.
    - **Solubility:** Haloalkanes are **slightly soluble in water** (a polar solvent) but **soluble in organic solvents** (non-polar solvents), following the principle of "like dissolves like".
3. **Optical Isomerism and Stereochemistry:**
    - **Chirality/Stereocenter:** Defining a **chiral center** (or asymmetric center) as an $sp^3$ hybridized atom bonded to **four different atoms or groups**. Chiral molecules are **optically active**.
    - **Optical Activity:** The ability of certain molecules to rotate the plane of **plane-polarized light** (measured by a **polarimeter**). Rotation is termed **dextrorotatory** (+) or **laevo-rotatory** (–).
    - **Enantiomers:** Stereoisomers that are **non-superimposable mirror images** of each other. They share identical physical properties but differ in the direction of optical rotation (equal and opposite).
    - **Racemic Mixture:** An **equimolar mixture of two enantiomers** resulting in **zero optical rotation** (optically inactive) due to external compensation.

### **Section 3: Methods of Preparation and Chemical Reactions of Haloalkanes**

This section focuses on the synthesis and core reactivity patterns of alkyl halides:

1. **Methods of Preparation (MOP):**
    - **From Alcohols:** Replacement of the $-\text{OH}$ group using hydrogen halides (HX), phosphorus halides ($\text{PX}_3, \text{PX}_5$), or thionyl chloride ($\text{SOCl}_2$). Tertiary alcohols are most reactive.
    - **From Hydrocarbons:** Free radical halogenation of alkanes, and addition of hydrogen halides or halogens across a double bond in alkenes (following Markovnikov’s rule for H–X addition).
    - **Halogen Exchange Reactions:** **Finkelstein reaction** ($\text{NaI}$ in acetone) for preparing alkyl iodides, and **Swarts reaction** (using metallic fluorides like $\text{AgF}$) for preparing alkyl fluorides.
2. **Chemical Reactions of Haloalkanes:**
    - **Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions ($\mathbf{S_N1}$ and $\mathbf{S_N2}$):**
        - **$\mathbf{S_N1}$ (Substitution Nucleophilic Unimolecular):** Proceeds in **two steps** via a **carbocation intermediate** (the slow, rate-determining step). The rate depends on **carbocation stability** ($3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ$). Leads to **racemization** (mixture of retention and inversion) because the planar carbocation can be attacked from either side.
        - **$\mathbf{S_N2}$ (Substitution Nucleophilic Bimolecular):** Occurs in a **single step** with both reactants determining the rate. The rate is hindered by **steric congestion** ($1^\circ > 2^\circ > 3^\circ$). Leads to **inversion of configuration** (Walden inversion), as the nucleophile attacks from the side opposite the leaving group.
        - **Ambident Nucleophiles:** Such as cyanide ($\text{KCN}$ vs $\text{AgCN}$), where the product depends on whether the reagent is ionic or covalent.
    - **Elimination Reactions ($\beta$-Elimination):** Heating a haloalkane with an **alcoholic solution of $\text{KOH}$** results in the elimination of $\text{H}$ and $\text{X}$ to form an alkene. When multiple alkenes can form, the major product is the **more substituted alkene** (Saytzeff’s Rule).
    - **Reactions with Metals:** Formation of **Grignard Reagents** ($\text{RMgX}$) using magnesium in **dry ether** (anhydrous conditions are necessary as Grignard reagents react violently with protons from water or alcohol). Also, **Wurtz reaction** (using sodium in dry ether to couple two alkyl halides, yielding an alkane with double the number of carbon atoms).

### **Section 4: Haloarenes and Polyhalogen Compounds**

This final section covers the chemistry specific to aryl halides and the environmental/industrial uses of polyhalogen compounds:

1. **Methods of Preparation (MOP) of Haloarenes:**
    - **Electrophilic Substitution:** Halogenation of arenes in the presence of a Lewis acid catalyst.
    - **From Diazonium Salts:** **Sandmeyer’s reaction** or **Gattermann reaction** replaces the diazonium group (generated from a primary aromatic amine) with $\text{Cl}$ or $\text{Br}$. **Balz-Schiemann reaction** is used for fluorination.
2. **Chemical Reactions of Haloarenes:**
    - **Nucleophilic Substitution:** Haloarenes are **extremely less reactive** toward nucleophilic substitution reactions due to **partial double bond character** (resonance effect), the greater electronegativity of the $sp^2$ hybridized carbon, and the instability of the phenyl cation. Substitution is only possible under **drastic conditions** (high temperature/pressure). The reactivity increases significantly if **electron-withdrawing groups** (like $-\text{NO}_2$) are present at the **ortho and para positions**.
    - **Electrophilic Substitution:** Haloarenes undergo typical benzene reactions (halogenation, nitration, etc.). The halogen atom is **ortho, para-directing** (due to the resonance effect), but is **deactivating** (due to the stronger $-\text{I}$ effect which withdraws electrons from the ring).
    - **Reactions with Metals:** **Fittig reaction** (coupling two aryl halides) and **Wurtz-Fittig reaction** (coupling an alkyl halide and an aryl halide).
3. **Polyhalogen Compounds:** A brief discussion on the industrial applications, environmental impact, and health effects of compounds such as **Dichloromethane**, **Chloroform** (used in $\text{R}-22$ refrigerant production, oxidizes to phosgene), **Iodoform**, **Carbon Tetrachloride** (depletes the ozone layer), **Freons**, and **DDT**.
