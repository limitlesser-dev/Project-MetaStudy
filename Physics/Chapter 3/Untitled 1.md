### Level 1: Fundamentals and Basic Definitions (The Building Blocks)

This level covers essential definitions, units, and simple concepts, often reviewed from prior classes, necessary to understand the subsequent physics. **Master these first.**

|Topic|Key Concepts and Formulas|Source References|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Electric Current ($I$)**|Defined as the rate of flow of charge: $I = \Delta Q / \Delta t$ or $I = dq/dt$. SI unit is Ampere (Coulomb per second).||
|**Electromotive Force (EMF, $\epsilon$) & Potential Difference ($V$)**|EMF is the work done per unit charge by the source in an open circuit (maximum potential difference). Potential difference is the work done per unit charge between two points. Both share the unit Volt (Joule per Coulomb).||
|**Conventional vs. Electronic Current**|Conventional current flows Positive to Negative (due to assumed positive charge movement). Electronic current flows Negative to Positive (due to actual electron movement). Electron flow is always opposite to conventional current direction.||
|**Resistance ($R$)**|Property of a substance that resists the flow of current. Resistance is the circuit's "friction". SI unit is Ohm ($\Omega$); $1\ \Omega = 1$ Volt per Ampere.||
|**Ohmâ€™s Law (Basic Statement)**|At constant temperature and dimensions, current flowing ($I$) is directly proportional to the potential difference ($V$) applied across its ends, resulting in $V = IR$.||
|**Power and Energy**|Power is the rate of doing work: $P = \text{Work Done} / \text{Time}$. Electric power formulas: $P = VI$, $P = I^2R$, or $P = V^2/R$.||
|**Commercial Unit of Energy**|Kilowatt-hour (kWh). $1$ kWh is equal to $36$ lakh Joules ($3.6 \times 10^6$ J).||

### Level 3: High-Priority Derivations and Complex Circuit Analysis (Crucial for 90% Marks)

These topics are frequently asked as full-fledged derivations, conceptual applications, or complex numerical problems (like those involving Kirchhoff's laws). **Dedicated study and practice are mandatory here.**

#### A. Derivations and Microscopic Theory

|Topic|Key Concepts and Formulas|Source References|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Relation between Current and Drift Velocity**|Derivation showing $I = n e A v_d$ (where $n$ is electron density, $e$ is charge, $A$ is area). Current is directly proportional to drift velocity.||
|**Drift Velocity Formula**|Derivation of $v_d = -e E \tau / m$. Shows $v_d$ is proportional to $E$.||
|**Microscopic Derivation of Ohm's Law**|**HIGH PRIORITY DERIVATION.** Substitute $v_d$ into the current relation to derive $V = IR$. This simultaneously proves $R \propto L/A$ and finds the resistivity formula: $\rho = m / (n e^2 \tau)$.||
|**Temperature Dependence of Resistivity/Resistance**|For conductors, increasing temperature increases resistance/resistivity. The linear relation is $R_T = R_0 [1 + \alpha (T - T_0)]$ or $\rho_T = \rho_0 [1 + \alpha (T - T_0)]$. $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity.||
|**EMF, Voltage, and Internal Resistance ($r$)**|Derivation showing the relationship between EMF ($\epsilon$), terminal voltage ($V$), current ($I$), and internal resistance ($r$): $V = \epsilon - Ir$ (discharging). Total current: $I = \epsilon / (R+r)$.||
|**EMF vs. Current Graph**|Plotting $V$ vs. $I$ yields a straight line with a negative slope, based on $V = -r I + \epsilon$ (comparing to $y=mx+c$). The slope is $-r$, and the y-intercept is $\epsilon$. **Know how to analyze this graph.**||

#### B. Circuit Solving and Advanced Laws

|Topic|Key Concepts and Formulas|Source References|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Kirchhoff's First Rule (Junction/Current Law)**|**HIGH PRIORITY PRINCIPLE.** The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum of currents leaving it ($\Sigma I_{\text{in}} = \Sigma I_{\text{out}}$). Based on the Law of Conservation of Charge.||
|**Kirchhoff's Second Rule (Loop/Voltage Law)**|**HIGH PRIORITY PRINCIPLE.** The algebraic sum of changes in potential around any closed loop is zero ($\Sigma \Delta V = 0$). This means total potential drop equals total potential gain. Based on the Law of Conservation of Energy. **Master the sign conventions** (e.g., traveling positive to negative terminal gives $-\epsilon$).||
|**Wheatstone Bridge**|**HIGH PRIORITY APPLICATION/PRINCIPLE.** Arrangement of five resistors used to determine an unknown resistance. **Master the Balancing Condition:** If the current through the galvanometer ($I_g$) is zero, then the ratio of adjacent resistances is equal: $P/Q = R/S$.||
|**Cells in Series and Parallel**|Series: $R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ...$; $\epsilon_{eq} = \epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2 + \epsilon_3 + ...$. Parallel: $1/R_{eq} = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + 1/R_3 + ...$; $\epsilon_{eq}/R_{eq} = \epsilon_1/R_1 + \epsilon_2/R_2 + \epsilon_3/R_3$.||
