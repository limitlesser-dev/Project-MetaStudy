### üìù Definition and Measurement of Electric Current ($I$)

Electric current is quantified by the ==rate at which charge passes through a cross-section== of a conductor:

#### 1. Fundamental Definition
Electric current is a ==**form of energy**== generated ==when charged particles come into motion==. It represents the amount of electric charge ($Q$) that ==**passes a point in a circuit per unit of time** ($t$).==

#### 2. Mathematical Formulation
For a steady current, the amount of current ($I$) is defined as ==the net charge ($q$ or $\Delta Q$) flowing across a cross-section during a time interval ($\Delta t$):==

$$I = \frac{q}{t} \quad \text{or} \quad I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

For non-steady currents, the current at time $t$ is the limit of this ratio as $\Delta t$ tends to zero, $I(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} (\Delta Q / \Delta t)$, often stated as the **rate of flow of charge** ($dQ/dt$).

#### 3. Units and Nature
*   **Unit:** The SI unit of current is the **ampere (A)**, named after Andr√©-Marie Amp√®re.
*   **Ampere Definition:** One ampere (1 A) is defined as **one coulomb of charge passing a point every second**.
*   **Nature:** Although current is often represented with a directed arrow, **it is a scalar quantity** because it does not obey the law of vector addition.

### ‚û°Ô∏è Directionality: Conventional vs. Electronic Current

The sources clearly state that conventional current and electronic current are **not different types of current**; they are two different ways of describing the direction of flow.

| Flow Type | Definition | Direction | Charge Carriers | Context |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Conventional Current** | The historical and standard convention for analysis. | **Positive terminal (+) to the negative terminal (-)**. | Assumed flow of positive charges. | Used universally in circuit diagrams and textbooks for consistency. |
| **Electronic Current** | The actual physical movement of the charge carriers. | **Negative terminal (-) to the positive terminal (+)**. | **Electrons** (negatively charged). | This direction is opposite to the conventional current because electrons are negative. |

In solid conductors like metal wires, the current is carried by negatively charged electrons. The **protons** (positive nuclei) remain tightly bound within the atomic structure and **do not move**.

###  classification of Current Types

Electric current is primarily classified into two distinct types based on directional constancy:

1.  **Direct Current (DC) üîã:**
    *   **Flow:** Flows continuously in **one constant direction** only.
    *   **Voltage:** Maintains a **steady, constant voltage** and zero frequency.
    *   **Examples:** Common sources include batteries and solar cells. DC is considered a **steady current**.

2.  **Alternating Current (AC) üîå:**
    *   **Flow:** **Periodically reverses its direction**.
    *   **Voltage:** Voltage varies as a sine wave.
    *   **Frequency:** The direction of flow changes back and forth many times per second (typically 50 or 60 Hz).
    *   **Usage:** Generated by power plants and used for household and industrial supply, mainly because it is easy to transform to different voltages for efficient long-distance transmission. AC is an example of a **non-steady current**.

### üî¨ Current in Conductors and Microscopic Basis

In conductors, the existence and magnitude of current are determined by internal properties, external electric fields, and resistance.

#### 1. Ohm's Law and Resistance
The most basic law governing current flow, discovered by G.S. Ohm in 1828, relates current ($I$) to potential difference ($V$):

*   **Proportionality:** The current flowing through a conductor is **directly proportional to the potential difference** applied across its ends ($V \propto I$).
*   **Resistance ($R$):** This relationship is expressed as $V = IR$, where $R$ is the constant of proportionality called **resistance**. $R$ is a property of the substance that resists the flow of current.
*   **Conditions:** Ohm's Law is valid only if the temperature and dimensions of the conductor are kept **constant**.
*   **Ohm's Law Limitations:** Ohm's law is ==not a fundamental law of nature== and fails if $V$ depends on $I$ ==non-linearly==, the relation depends on the sign of $V$ (like in a ==diode==), or the relation is non-unique (like in ==GaAs==).

#### 2. Drift Velocity ($v_d$) and Current Mechanism
When an electric field ($E$) is applied across a conductor, the electrons move, but not smoothly.

*   **Thermal Motion:** In the absence of $E$, electrons move randomly due to thermal energy, resulting in **zero net average velocity** and zero net current.
*   **Drift Phenomenon:** When $E$ is applied, electrons are accelerated, but they suffer frequent **collisions with fixed positive ions**. These collisions cause them to lose their drift speed, only to accelerate and collide again.
*   **Steady Average Velocity:** Due to these collisions, electrons acquire a **steady average velocity** called the **drift velocity** ($v_d$), which is independent of time. This velocity is typically extremely small (e.g., a few mm/s).
*   **Current Equation:** Current ($I$) is directly related to the drift velocity ($v_d$), the number density of free electrons ($n$), the charge of the electron ($e$), and the cross-sectional area ($A$):
    $$I = n e A |v_d|$$
*   **Instantaneous Current:** Although the actual electron drift speed is slow, the establishment of the electric field signal propagates at the speed of light, causing current to be established almost instantly throughout the circuit when it is closed.

#### 3. Current Density ($j$)
Current density is a vector quantity that describes the flow locally.

*   **Definition:** Current density ($j$) is the **current flowing per unit area** normal (perpendicular) to the flow.
*   **Formula and Units:** $j = I/A$. The SI unit is $\text{A/m}^2$.
*   **Direction:** The vector $\mathbf{j}$ is directed along the electric field $\mathbf{E}$.

#### 4. Relation to Potential Difference (EMF)
To maintain a steady current ($I$), an external source (like a cell or battery) is required to continuously move charge from a lower to a higher potential energy level.

*   **EMF ($\epsilon$):** Electromotive force (EMF) is the potential difference between the two terminals of the source in an **open circuit** (when no current is flowing).
*   **Terminal Voltage ($V$):** When the source is connected to an external resistance ($R$) and current ($I$) flows, the terminal voltage is given by $V = \epsilon - I r$, where $r$ is the cell's **internal resistance**. The internal resistance is the resistance of the cell or battery itself.

In summary, electric current is the flow of charge, quantified in amperes, and its steady state behavior in circuits is governed by Ohm's Law and its microscopic origin tied to the drift motion of charge carriers against the resistance offered by the material.
