### ⚡ Current Electricity: Detailed Checklist

#### I. Fundamentals and Definitions

- - [x] Transition from Electrostatics (Charges at rest) to **Electrodynamics** (Charges in motion).
- - [x] Definition of **Electricity** (Form of energy due to flow of charge particles).
- - [x] Definition of **Electric Current ($I$)** (Rate of flow of electric charge per unit time).
- - [x] Current Formulas:
    
    - - [x] $I = Q/t$ (for steady current).
    - - [x] $I = \Delta Q / \Delta t$.
    - - [x] $I(t) = dQ/dt$ (instantaneous current).
- - [x] SI Unit of Current: **Ampere (A)**.
- - [x] Definition of 1 Ampere (1 Coulomb of charge passing per second).
- - [ ] **Charge Carriers:**
    
    - - [x] In Solid Conductors (Metals): Electrons (negative charge carriers).
    - - [ ] In Electrolytes/Plasma: Both positive and negative ions/charges can move.
- - [x] **Current Direction Conventions:**
    
    - - [ ] **Conventional Current:** Direction of assumed positive charge flow (Positive terminal $\to$ Negative terminal).
    - - [ ] **Electronic Current (Electron Flow):** Actual physical movement (Negative terminal $\to$ Positive terminal).

#### II. Types of Current and Flow Characteristics

- - [x] **Steady Current** (Value does not change with time).
- - [x] **Non-Steady/Varying Current** (Value fluctuates with time).
- - [x] **Direct Current (DC):**
    
    - - [ ] Flows continuously in one constant direction.
    - - [ ] Maintains constant voltage and zero frequency.
    - - [ ] Sources: Batteries, solar cells.
- - [x] **Alternating Current (AC):**
    
    - - [ ] Periodically reverses its direction.
    - - [ ] Voltage varies as a sine wave (e.g., 50 or 60 Hz).
    - - [ ] Efficient for long-distance transmission via voltage transformation.
- - [x] Charge flow mechanism in conductors (random motion vs. motion under E-field).

#### III. Voltage, EMF, and Internal Resistance

- - [ ] **Potential Difference (Voltage, $V$):** Energy or "push" that moves electrons.
- - [ ] **Electromotive Force (EMF, $\epsilon$):**
    
    - - [ ] Defined for the source (cell/battery).
    - - [ ] Maximum Potential Difference (voltage in an open circuit).
    - - [ ] Formula: $\epsilon = \text{Work Done} / \text{Charge}$.
- - [ ] **Internal Resistance ($r$):** Resistance within the cell or battery.
- - [ ] **Terminal Voltage ($V$):**
    
    - - [ ] During Discharging: $V = \epsilon - I r$.
    - - [ ] During Charging: $V = \epsilon + I r$.
- - [ ] **Total Current ($I$)** in a circuit with internal resistance: $I = \epsilon / (R + r)$.
- - [ ] **V vs I Graph (Discharge):** Straight line sloping downward; Slope is $-r$ (negative internal resistance) and Y-intercept is $\epsilon$ (EMF).

#### IV. Ohm’s Law, Resistance, and Resistivity

- - [x] **Ohm’s Law:** $V \propto I$ or $V = IR$ (at constant temperature and dimensions).
- - [ ] **Resistance ($R$):** Constant of proportionality that opposes current flow.
- - [ ] Unit of Resistance: **Ohm ($\Omega$)**.
- - [ ] **Factors Governing Resistance:**
    
    - - [ ] Directly proportional to Length ($R \propto L$).
    - - [ ] Inversely proportional to Area of Cross-Section ($R \propto 1/A$).
- - [ ] **Resistance Formula:** $R = \rho L/A$.
- - [ ] **Resistivity ($\rho$):** Material property (specific resistance) independent of dimensions.
- - [ ] Unit of Resistivity: **Ohm meter ($\Omega$ m)**.
- - [ ] **Classification by Resistivity:**
    
    - - [ ] Conductors (low $\rho$).
    - - [ ] Insulators (high $\rho$).
    - - [ ] Semiconductors (intermediate $\rho$).
- - [ ] **Temperature Dependence of Resistivity:**
    
    - - [ ] Formula: $\rho_T = \rho_0 [1 + \alpha (T-T_0)]$.
    - - [ ] **Conductors:** Resistivity increases with temperature ($\alpha$ positive).
    - - [ ] **Semiconductors:** Resistivity decreases with temperature.
    - - [ ] **Alloys (Nichrome/Manganin):** Weak temperature dependence (used for standard resistors).

#### V. Microscopic Theory and Vector Forms

- - [ ] **Current Density ($\vec{J}$):** Current flowing per unit area normal to flow ($J = I/A$).
    
    - - [ ] Unit: $A/m^2$.
    - - [ ] Vector relationship: $I = \vec{J} \cdot \vec{A}$.
- - [ ] **Ohm’s Law (Vector Form):** $\vec{E} = \rho \vec{J}$ or $\vec{J} = \sigma \vec{E}$.
- - [ ] **Conductivity ($\sigma$):** Reciprocal of resistivity ($\sigma = 1/\rho$).
- - [ ] **Drift Velocity ($v_d$):** Average velocity acquired by electrons in the direction opposite to $\vec{E}$.
    
    - - [ ] Formula: $v_d = -e E \tau / m$.
- - [ ] **Relaxation Time ($\tau$):** Average time interval between successive collisions of an electron.
- - [ ] **Relation between Current and Drift Velocity:** $I = n e A v_d$.
- - [ ] **Microscopic Resistivity Formula:** $\rho = m / (n e^2 \tau)$.
- - [ ] **Mobility ($\mu$):** Magnitude of drift velocity per unit electric field applied ($\mu = |v_d| / E$).
    
    - - [ ] Formula: $\mu = e \tau / m$.
    - - [ ] Relation to Current: $I = n e A \mu E$.

#### VI. Electrical Energy and Power

- - [ ] **Energy Dissipated (Work Done):** $\Delta W = I V \Delta t$.
- - [ ] **Electric Power ($P$):** Rate of electrical energy dissipation.
- - [ ] **Power Formulas (Ohmic Loss):**
    
    - - [ ] $P = V I$.
    - - [ ] $P = I^2 R$.
    - - [ ] $P = V^2 / R$.
- - [ ] **Joule’s Law of Heating:** Heat produced $H = I^2 R T$.
- - [ ] **Applications of Heating Effect:**
    
    - - [ ] Electric Fuse (Melts due to high current, protecting circuit).
    - - [ ] Electric Bulb (Tungsten filament glows due to heat, uses inert gases).
    - - [ ] Heating Appliances (Uses Nichrome alloy due to high resistance).
- - [ ] **Commercial Unit of Energy:** Kilowatt-hour (kW h or "unit").
    
    - - [ ] Conversion: $1 \text{ kW h} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ Joules}$.

#### VII. Circuit Combinations

- - [ ] **Resistors in Series:** $R_{net} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots$ (Current is same).
- - [ ] **Resistors in Parallel:** $1/R_{net} = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + 1/R_3 + \dots$ (Voltage is same).
- - [ ] **Cells in Series:**
    
    - - [ ] Equivalent EMF: $\epsilon_{eq} = \epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2 + \dots$ (if connected positive to negative).
    - - [ ] Equivalent Internal Resistance: $r_{eq} = r_1 + r_2 + \dots$.
    - - [ ] Alternative EMF: $\epsilon_{eq} = \epsilon_1 - \epsilon_2$ (if terminals are reversed).
- - [ ] **Cells in Parallel:**
    
    - - [ ] Equivalent Internal Resistance: $1/r_{eq} = 1/r_1 + 1/r_2 + \dots$.
    - - [ ] Equivalent EMF Relation: $\epsilon_{eq} / r_{eq} = \epsilon_1 / r_1 + \epsilon_2 / r_2 + \dots$.

#### VIII. Kirchhoff’s Rules and Wheatstone Bridge

- - [ ] **Kirchhoff's Rules** (Used for complex circuits).
- - [ ] **Junction Rule (First Law):**
    
    - - [ ] Statement: Sum of currents entering a junction equals sum of currents leaving ($\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$).
    - - [ ] Basis: Law of Conservation of Charge.
- - [ ] **Loop Rule (Second Law):**
    
    - - [ ] Statement: Algebraic sum of changes in potential around any closed loop is zero ($\sum \Delta V = 0$).
    - - [ ] Basis: Law of Conservation of Energy.
    - - [ ] Sign Conventions (Potential Drop/Gain across R and $\epsilon$).
- - [ ] **Wheatstone Bridge:** Arrangement of four resistors ($R_1, R_2, R_3, R_4$) and a galvanometer ($G$).
    
    - - [ ] Battery Arm (Source connection, e.g., AC).
    - - [ ] Galvanometer Arm (e.g., BD).
    - - [ ] **Balancing Condition (Null Point):** Current through galvanometer ($I_G$) is zero.
    - - [ ] Balance Formula: $R_1 / R_2 = R_3 / R_4$ or $R_2 / R_1 = R_4 / R_3$.
    - - [ ] Practical Application: Determining unknown resistance (Meter Bridge).

#### IX. Limitations of Ohm’s Law and Special Materials

- - [ ] **Ohmic Conductors:** Follow Ohm's Law (e.g., Metals).
- - [ ] **Non-Ohmic Conductors:** Do not follow Ohm's Law (e.g., PN Junction, GaAs).
- - [ ] **Conditions/Limitations where Ohm's Law Fails:**
    
    - - [ ] $V$ is non-linearly proportional to $I$.
    - - [ ] Relation between $V$ and $I$ depends on the sign of $V$ (e.g., Diode).
    - - [ ] Relation between $V$ and $I$ is non-unique (e.g., GaAs).
- - [ ] **Superconductivity:** Phenomenon of complete loss of resistivity ($\rho \approx 0$).
    
    - - [ ] **Critical Temperature ($T_C$):** Temperature below which a material becomes superconducting.
- - [ ] **Meissner Effect:** Active expulsion of magnetic field lines by a superconductor when cooled below $T_C$.
